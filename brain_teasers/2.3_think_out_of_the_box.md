```

problem, you have a 8 x 8 chess board with two small squares at the opposite diagonal
comers removed. You have many bricks with dimension 1 x 2. Can you pack 31 bricks
into the remaining 62 squares? (An alternative question is whether you can cover all 62
squares using bricks without any bricks overlapping with each other or sticking out of
the board, which requires a similar analysis.)

8 * 8 = 64, and 32 is Black and 32 is white

if we remove the opposite diagonal: they are the same color!

so 32 black and 30 white

one brick 1 x 2 is using 1 B and 1 W, so 31 bricks cannot fit 30 white , so the answer is not possible.

we can leverage the similar thinking: imaging 6 * 6 * 6 is full of 2 * 2 * 2 cubes: it will b  3^3 = 27 cubes. 

Let's color the cubes to black and white alternatively: 13 black 2x2 （layer by layer 5, 4, 5） and 14 white 2x2.
```

# Door to offer
You are facing two doors. One leads to your job offer and the other leads to exit. In front
of either door is a guard. One guard always tells lies and the other always tells the truth.
You can only ask **one guard one yes/no question**. Assuming you do want to get the job
offer, what question will you ask?

```
solution: 

two situations:
A:
offer & lie
exit & truth
B:
offer & truth
exit & lie

if we ask "is your door to offer? "
A:
offer & lie -> No
exit & truth -> No
B:
offer & truth -> yes
exit & lie -> yes
we cannot tell which door is good. 

so we need a question to make a difference between the lier and the truth talker, direct question will always be the same answer and we cannot get information. 

The key is to involve both guards in the questions as the popular answer does.

`Will the other guard tell me the yes, if I ask your door is offer?`

A:
offer & lie -> no: the truth talker will say No, because i'm lying
exit & truth -> yes: the lier will tell you my door is offer
B:
offer & truth -> no: the lier will say my door is not offer
exit & lie -> yes: the truth talker will tell you my door is offer, in fact it is not.
```
