


# box packing
Can you pack 53 bricks of dimensions 1x1x4 into a 6 x 6 x 6 box?

solution: 
```
This is a nice problem extended from a popular chess board problem. In that
problem, you have a 8 x 8 chess board with two small squares at the opposite diagonal
comers removed. You have many bricks with dimension 1 x 2. Can you pack 31 bricks
into the remaining 62 squares? (An alternative question is whether you can cover all 62
squares using bricks without any bricks overlapping with each other or sticking out of
the board, which requires a similar analysis.)

8 * 8 = 64, and 32 is Black and 32 is white

if we remove the opposite diagonal: they are the same color!

so 32 black and 30 white

one brick 1 x 2 is using 1 B and 1 W, so 31 bricks cannot fit 30 white , so the answer is not possible. 

theoritical: `6 * 6 * 6 = 36 * 6 = 36 * + 180 = 216` vs `53 * 4 = 212`

we can leverage the similar thinking: imaging 6 * 6 * 6 is full of 2 * 2 * 2 cubes: it will b  3^3 = 27 cubes. 

Let's color the cubes to black and white alternatively: 13 black 2x2 （layer by layer 5, 4, 5） and 14 white 2x2.  

So every brick of 1x1x4 will take 2 1x1 black and 2 1x1 white, nomatter how the position is. then 53 brisks will take 53*2 = 106 black and 106 white

How many black 1x1 cube  do we have: 13*4 = 52, so no space for 53 bricks.

# Door to offer
You are facing two doors. One leads to your job offer and the other leads to exit. In front
of either door is a guard. One guard always tells lies and the other always tells the truth.
You can only ask **one guard one yes/no question**. Assuming you do want to get the job
offer, what question will you ask?

```
solution: 

two situations:
A:
offer & lie
exit & truth
B:
offer & truth
exit & lie

if we ask "is your door to offer? "
A:
offer & lie -> No
exit & truth -> No
B:
offer & truth -> yes
exit & lie -> yes
we cannot tell which door is good. 

so we need a question to make a difference between the lier and the truth talker, direct question will always be the same answer and we cannot get information. 

The key is to involve both guards in the questions as the popular answer does.

`Will the other guard tell me the yes, if I ask your door is offer?`

A:
offer & lie -> no: the truth talker will say No, because i'm lying
exit & truth -> yes: the lier will tell you my door is offer
B:
offer & truth -> no: the lier will say my door is not offer
exit & lie -> yes: the truth talker will tell you my door is offer, in fact it is not.
```

# Last ball

(Assume each ball in the bag has equal probability of being taken). You do not put these
two balls back. Instead, if both balls have the same color, you add a blue ball to the bag;
if they have different colors, you add a red ball to the bag. Assume that you have an
unlimited supply of blue and red balls, if you keep on repeating this process, what will
be the color of the last ball left in the bag?  What if the bag has 20 blue balls and 13 red
balls instead?

```
solution:
blue = 20;
red = 14;

[20, 14]
 p(br||rb) = 2*20*14/34*33 -> red [19, 14]
 p(bb)=20/34 * 19/33 -> blue [19, 14]
 p(rr)=14/34*13/33 -> blue [21, 12]

[b,r]
 p(br||rb)-> red [ b-1, r]
 p(bb) -> blue [ b-1, r]
 p(rr) -> blue [b+1, r-2]

b can +1 or -1, r can stay or -2, and the total is always -1, so in the end , we have only one left,
and r start from 14, with all -2, cannot be 1, so the last one must be blue.

# Quant salary
Eight quants from different banks are getting together for drinks. They are all interested
in knowing the average salary of the group. Nevertheless, being cautious and humble
individuals, everyone prefers not to disclose his or her own salary to the group. Can you
come up with a strategy for the quants to calculate the average salary without knowing
other people's salaries?

```
avg means we need the sum we have the number, do we have a way to get sum anonymously?
add a random number:
the first quant to choose a random number, adds it to his/her salary and gives it to the
second quant. The second quant will add his/her own salary to the result and give it to
the third quant; ... ; the eighth quant will add his/her own salary to the result and give it
back to the first quant. Then the first quant will deduct the "random" number from the
total and divide the "real" total by 8 to yield the average salary.
```
